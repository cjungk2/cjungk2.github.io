Introduction ¶

In the context of signficant political and economic changes, the global state of well-being has become increasingly important. In our report, we wanted to analyze the factors that may or may not contribute to a country's happiness. In order to do this, we have decided to use the World Happiness Report, which analyzes data from 2015 to 2019 from over 170 countries. This dataset provides many features and describes the extent to which it can impact a country's happiness. This includes GDP per Capita, Family, Life Expectancy, Freedom, Generosity, Trust Government Corruption.

With this dataset, our primary explorations which of these features would have the strongest correlation with happiness. In conjunction, we also explore whether or not happiness would vary by year or by region. Additionally, we compare the predictive performance between linear regression and random forest models in estimating happiness scores.

Through these explorations we hope to provide insight for governments and policy makers on ways to objectively increase the quality of life, well-being, and happiness on their respective countries.

Data Curation ¶

Source and Explanation ¶

As previously mentioned, we used the World Happiness Report dataset. This dataset is based on Gallup World Poll data from 2015 to 2019 from 170 countries, ranking each country by happiness score using the Cantril ladder. The Cantril ladder asks the respondent to rate their current life out of 10 where the best possible life is a 10 and the worst possible life is a 0. The values of each of the six factors (GDP per capita, social support, healthy life expectancy, freedom, perceptions of corruption, and generosity) are estimates of the extent that the given factor contributes to the happiness score. By the end of the parsing and organizing, the columns we are left with are:

  • Country
  • Region
  • Happiness Score
  • GDP per Capita
  • Social Support
  • Healthy Life Expectancy
  • Freedom
  • Perceptions of Corruption
  • Generosity
  • Year

First Hypothesis (Correlation Between GDP per Capita and Happiness Score) ¶

Assume $\alpha = 0.05$.
HO: The GDP per Capita does not have an effect on happiness score.
HA: The GDP per Capita does have an effect on happiness score.

A visualization of the hypothesis.

In [426]:
plt.scatter(happiness_df['GDP_per_Capita'], happiness_df['Happiness_Score'])
plt.xlabel('GDP per Capita')
plt.ylabel('Happiness Score')
plt.title('GDP per Capita vs Happiness Score')
plt.show()

We will use Pearson's correlation coefficient for this hypothesis test because we are dealing with continuous variables, and our previous scatter plot suggests that there is a linear relationship.

For a deeper understanding of Pearson's correlation coefficient: https://www.geeksforgeeks.org/pearson-correlation-coefficient/

In [428]:
result = pearsonr(happiness_df['GDP_per_Capita'], happiness_df['Happiness_Score'])
print(f'P-value: {result.pvalue}')

P-value: 2.213787835429655e-167

Since the p-value is less than our significance level of 0.05, we reject the null hypothesis, concluding that the GDP per Capita does have an effect on happiness score.

Third Hypothesis (Comparing Happiness Score between 2015 and 2019 (two sample t-test)) ¶

Assume $\alpha = 0.05$.
HO: The mean Happiness Score in 2019 is equal to that in 2015.
HA: The mean Happiness Score in 2019 is not equal to that in 2015.

In [437]:
data_2015 = happiness_df[happiness_df['Year'] == 2015]['Happiness_Score'].dropna()
data_2019 = happiness_df[happiness_df['Year'] == 2019]['Happiness_Score'].dropna()

t_stat, p_val = ttest_ind(data_2019, data_2015, equal_var=False)

print("Hypothesis Test: Comparing Happiness Score (2019 vs 2015)")
print("Null Hypothesis (H0): μ_2019 = μ_2015")
print("Alternative Hypothesis (H1): μ_2019 ≠ μ_2015")
print("T-statistic:", t_stat)
print("P-value:", p_val)

if p_val < 0.05:
    print("Result: Reject H0. There is a statistically significant difference in Happiness Scores between 2019 and 2015.")
else:
    print("Result: Fail to reject H0. There is no statistically significant difference in Happiness Scores between 2019 and 2015.")

Fourth Hypothesis (Effect of Region on Happiness Score) ¶

Assume $\alpha = 0.05$.
HO: Region does not have an effect on the Happiness Score of a country.
HA: Region does have an effect on the Happiness Score of a country.

Visually, it looks like the regions make a difference. Let us do an ANOVA test to make sure.

Insights and Conclusions ¶

  1. Wealth is a Large Factor for Happiness

Across the many countries in the study, it is clear that wealth is a large factor. The Pearson correlation between GDP per capita and Happiness Score remains effectively zero, with $p \approx 2.2 * 10^{-167}$ ($r \approx 0.79$), showcasing an extremely strong linear relationship. In our Random Forest model, GDP per capita alone accounts for approximately 51% of the overall predictive weight, more than the next three predictors combined, showcasing that without sufficient material resources, improvements elsewhere do not matter as much to change subjective well‑being. At the policy level, this might mean that raising average incomes through employment, education, or social protection is likely the single most important lever for raising a nation’s happiness.

  1. Health and Autonomy

Other than money, two other factors are also noticeably dominant. Healthy life expectancy is the second most important predictor (approximately 20% importance), indicating that healthier, longer lives reduce pressure and allow people to enjoy the fruits of their work. Freedom to choose in life is third (approximately 9% importance), showing the value of civil liberties. These factors go hand in hand, healthy individuals can better use their freedoms, and freedom to make good choices encourages healthier living.

  1. Social Support

Social support is also important. Although it accounts for only about 4% of the Random Forest’s power, its Pearson correlation with Happiness Score in 2019 is $r \approx 0.78$ with ($p \approx 9.0 * 10^{-33}$), showing a strong association. This implies that community programs, mental‑health services, and civic‑engagement policies can add substantial benefits in well‑being.

  1. Regional Differences

The one‑way ANOVA shows a highly significant difference in mean Happiness Scores across world regions ($p \approx 9.8 * 10^{-155}$). Western Europe and North America average around 7.0 on the 0–10 scale, while Sub‑Saharan Africa and South Asia average around 4.5. However, when region is included alongside GDP, health, and social support in the Random Forest, all regional variables each contribute less than 1.5% importance (bar Latin America and Caribbean). This shows that “region” is not a root cause but a byproduct for clusters of economic and social conditions. Regional development efforts should target more income, health systems, and social infrastructure rather than geography.

  1. Happiness over Time

The 2015 vs. 2019 two‑sample t‑test comparison yields $t \approx 0.25$, $p \approx 0.81$, indicating no statistically significant change in the global average Happiness Score (both years are approximately 5.3). This highlights the importance of examining country‑level trends, since a stable world average could hide major country specific shifts.
